72 hours of Vipassana

What is this?

As a disclaimer of sorts, many of the mishaps in this essay could and would have been dealt with by a slightly more in-depth Google search prior to signing up. Unfortunately, I’m more of a learn-on-the-job, throw away the instructions, and find-out-the-hard way kind of guy. Therefore, this essay intends not to reprimand the course or those who take it, but to document and explore my findings and experience. This should be read with a strong preface that I expect my doubts and criticisms to have valid answers that an assistant teacher could give me.

What I expected vs What I found

My expectations of the course were far from what I found. I signed up for this Vipassana because I wanted the most extreme immersion into (what I thought was) mediation, as quickly as possible. The course upheld its part as extreme emersion, albeit its version of meditation proved far from my misconception. Vipassana is an exercise in which one observes their own breathing as a process of “mental purification” (excuse my omission of nuance). It is intended to “eradicate suffering”, and allow us to “face life’s tensions” in a calm, balanced way. At my own fault, I was expecting the experience to facilitate deep thinking. I wanted an exploration of ideas deep within, with the possibility of merging into some spiritual thought-lane, where everyone drives at the speed of genius. The reality is that the technique focuses on exactly the opposite, rather than joining the thought high-way, the objective is to take refuge in the thought-less layby. The technique focused on the ability to let thoughts pass you by - granting a measured approach to life’s problems with a clear, empty mind. Whilst this is no wasted endeavour, it was most definitely a diversion from my expected journey. Where I was hoping for insight and exploration, I found a problem-solving tool.

Vipassana and Christianity

As a non-devout Christian, I couldn’t help but draw comparisons in the Vipassana. Firstly in Vipassana, our minds are impure whereas in Christianity we are sinners. This fundamental difference is that an impure mind implies an innate issue, the person - you - are defiled. A sinner however can be a good person, who has sinned. Therefore, sin is easier to reconcile as it is surface level. A sin can be corrected like cutting out a cancerous cell, without believing that the entire being is corrupted.

A second comparison is the pattern of teacher-vessel-assistant teacher. In Christianity, we have the teacher (God), the vessel (Gospel) and the assistant teacher (the Priest) delivering the teachings. In the Vipassana, we also have the teacher (S.N. Goenka), the vessel (his video course) and the assistant teacher - the literal assistant teachers on the course. The difference in vessel is of particular interest as a book (e.g. The Bible) acts as a veil of mystery as opposed to seeing and hearing the teacher through Goenka’s videos. The veil of mystery - and absence of it - has interesting implications for the attributes we place on the teacher. In the Vipassana case, I can see that S.N. Goenka is a human just like you or I, who has brought this technique to the forefront - seeing this human form teacher makes it easier to question teachings, which I found difficult as during the course, his word his final. When the source is mysterious and not explicitly human (i.e. The Gospel and God) then teachings are easier to digest as some worldly eternal truth.

Independent thinking and selective participation

Religious practices are most valuable to the individual when they allow for well-meaning discourse and selective participation, i.e. one can outwardly question commandments and then choose which to accept and decline. I will happily subscribe to the Christian belief of “loving thy neighbour” - however remarks on homosexuality or premarital intercourse are postal orders which I choose not to return. The argument of whether it is fair, possible, or morally sufficient to engage in selective participation is tangential and not addressed in this essay. Its relevance to this essay is that simply having the freedom of debate and selective participation makes religious practice more palatable - and was absent in the Vipassana experience. So how do we grant this freedom? Environment. Whether in Christian school, speaking with members of the Parish, or even with friends and family - debate and exploration of Christian values have always been encouraged in environments I’ve been a part of (I’m aware this mightn’t be the norm). Openly my non-religious friends will challenge various aspects of Christianity, and often I concur. The issue with the structure of the Vipassana course is that there simply isn’t a forum to facilitate this discourse. Attendees can not pursue exploration of the practice but instead must embody all prescribed values. Failure to do so is the deceit of your impure mind. Albeit the schedule allows for a one-on-one interview with an assistant teacher, that conversation bears more resemblance to a toddler questioning “why” and the all-knowing parent answering “because I said so”. Answers in the interview are presented as so true and undeniable that querying their origin is frankly disrespectful (after all, you are the student who is here to learn). Any debate around them is shrouded by the looming fact that your mobile phone, valuables, and any means of autonomy are placed firmly within a guarded locker to which your debate partner (and honorary prison warden) holds the key.

Leaving so soon?

Unsurprisingly the consistent thread amongst the multitude of reasons for my early departure was a misalignment of beliefs between S.N.Goenka and I. And that’s okay. Here is a whirl-wind tour of my understanding of our differences and why I felt it was best to leave:

  1. Internalising an “Impure Mind” mindset

Internalizing the idea that our minds are impure seems dangerous. Instructions read that the Vipassana is a “method of mental purification” and the teacher explains how our minds are impure. If you have ever experienced anger or harmful emotions, then you are guilty of an impure mind. We have all experienced these emotions. Do you wish to internalise the mindset that you have an impure mind? It seems that this perpetuates a negative self-image and I’m not sure this is conducive for a happier way of being.

  1. Where’s the good?

The way in which the technique helps people deal with life’s problems is through detachment. You become indifferent to the outcomes of situations, meaning they can not disappoint you (Stoicism overlap?). I agree that this is an effective tool in dealing with hardships, however it comes at the cost of being able to enjoy positive outcomes. For example, you are waiting to hear back from a company regarding a job offer. To be truly indifferent (and thus avoid suffering) you will be unattached as to whether you receive the offer or not. Of course, this is helpful if you don’t receive the offer - you are indifferent, you don’t care and it simply happened. However in the case that you do receive the offer, are you able to celebrate it? Are you able to enjoy it? Is a positive reaction to the offer an admission that you were never truly indifferent? I find it difficult to believe that you can simultaneously be truly indifferent and enjoy positive outcomes.

  1. What’s life without feeling?

If you can be truly indifferent and unattached - isn’t life for feeling anyway? I recognise that life has highs and lows, however surely to feel the highs you must also feel the lows. I see how employing a detached mindset can be useful in the event of something unfortunate happening. However, if you are to live your life embodying this mindset, then are you not also embodying a life with the omission of emotion? Moving through as a soulless robot? This isn’t the kind of life I wish to lead.

  1. Connected or Distinct from Mr World

A fourth misalignment is a view towards being connected to the world around us. The previously mentioned indifference extends to the world around us. Rain? Unattached. Sunshine? Unattached. I want to live a life connected to the world around me and the emotions which accompany it.

  1. So this is what enlightenment looks like?

The Vipassana process struck me as very strange. In this compound, you have 35 other men, walking and sitting around in silence. If you were to look at their faces (against the rules) without context, you might think you were looking at a wake or the aftermath of a zombie apocalypse (the kind in the movies where the team won, but in doing so they had to kill the zombifications of their loved ones and the post-battle stillness allows for the dawning of such truth). The discomfort of sitting for hours on end was “part of the game” and seemed masochistic in nature, despite what the teacher claimed. I didn’t expect the route to enlightenment or the path to happiness to be so depressing.

When I met the people who had been through the process and were living a life embodying these values, I found they were not people I wanted to emulate. These people were the course leader, the assistant course leader, and the assistant teacher (the teacher was the video-taped 1991 Mr Goenka). They carried an air of unease. I couldn’t feel quite relaxed around them, as if there was an underlying tension beneath a covering of peace and serenity. This could definitely be untrue and could be my biased perspective - but I can only operate within my experienced reality.

  1. Do you want a silenced mind?

I’m not sure a silenced mind is a good thing (for me at least). I have benefited from the exercises, noting their improvement on my focus. However, what is a silenced mind and is that something you want? Yes, free from anxieties, reduced stress and suffering are all amazing selling points. However, does this come at the cost of innovative ideas, sparks of inspiration, and self-discovery? Each person’s trade-off will undoubtedly differ.

  1. Weak Whys Make Weak Tries

I did not have a strong motivation whilst entering the experience. From brief discussions with coursemates prior to our “noble silence” commencing and from observing during the course, I concluded that most people were at the course as a remedy of some kind. Depression, illness, stress; the diagnosis can vary but the cure remains the same. Alternatively, I was there out of curiosity. I was interested in the practice and joining from a positive place. Perhaps this technique is most effective for the people who need it and I am simply not yet the target audience.

A cult-worthy exit

I’m expecting this essay to take a dim view of the Vipassana. I don’t think this is fair. Evidently the course helps thousands to alleviate their suffering and approach life in a way which feels more manageable to them. This is wonderful and I (perhaps surprisingly) support it. The reason for the bias in this essay is the cult-like process of exiting the course. Skipping details, it took five separate occasions of asking the course manager if I could leave in order to be allowed to meet the teacher (i.e. the final barrier to an untimely exit). Then on meeting the teacher, you are faced with a skilled and experienced debater in all of the reasons why your notion to leave is invalid. Your body is hurting? That is part of the game! You disagree with the beliefs? This is your mind playing tricks on you! You want to leave? So did I in your position, but look at how far (and enlightened) I’ve become! Honestly, I don’t mean to tastelessly castigate the Vipassana course. However, this type of exit cannot help but leave a sour taste in the mouth. Whilst uncertain whether it was made intentionally difficult to leave or not (i.e. was this simply poor management and a difficult teacher?), and whether those intentions were malicious or not (i.e. I can’t see any malevolent benefit beyond a marginal monetary gain from my staying, unless the teachers truly believed I had more to gain - which I’m certain they do) - I do believe that in order to avoid cult accusations, a more seamless bowing-out process could be helpful.
